The Original Memes

Memes Are More Than The Modern Usage Of The Term

Richard Dawkins: Creator of "Meme"

In his 1976 book, The Selfish Gene, Dawkins coined the term meme from the Greek Root "mimeme".

As an evolutionary biologist studying genes, he was looking for a way to describe and analyze the way cultural information evolves as it is passed on to other people. He created the original definition of meme and defined it as “a unit of cultural transmission” and as “a unit of imitation”1. Memes are considered a form of imitation because of the way people repeat the information given to them or copy what they see others doing. The emphasis is strongly on cultural or traditional behaviors that people pass to other people, so some forms of memes are ideas, tunes, catch phrases, images, recipes, and dances2.

Kilroy Was Here!

Kilroy was graffitied across the world by US soldiers throughout WWII.

Folklore can be seen as some of the original memes because people creatively “remix” the stories as they imitate and pass them on throughout generations, thus making it an ever evolving transmission of culture3. No one is clear where Kilroy originated, making it even more of a meme because the various iterations of its backstory indicate cultural engagement with the image. Kilroy represented “the fighting spirit of American soldiers” as a symbol of hope4.

Frodo Lives

This catchphrase based meme was part of 1960s-70’s hippie counterculture.

It was a reference to Frodo Baggins from Lord of the Rings being sent on a death mission to Mordor, and it became a symbol for “protesters fighting for marginalized peoples [because they] found great inspiration in revolution by the ‘little people' (the Hobbits)”4. It was imitated and spread onto pins, stickers, posters, shirts, and more. It is considered a meme due to it becoming widespread and because it was a symbol for the cultural values.

Dancing Baby: First Internet Meme

First viral form of the modern definition of meme is “Dancing Baby” from 1996.

Started from a gif to show off Kinetix Character Studio’s new CGI abilities, Dancing Baby was a 3D animated diapered baby whose unsettling looking dancing abilities quickly spread across the internet. It even aired on CNN in 1998, where the newscast had to actually define what a meme was because viral memes were such a new concept. Dancing Baby serves as a good demonstration for where digital memes are rooted, and it creates an interesting point of reference to compare current memes back to.

The Memeification of Politics

Memes have evolved even further to become useful tools of influence, including a social commentary that’s comparable to political cartoons. Political memes can be humorous and sardonic, just like political cartoons; however, it can be argued that political memes have an advantage in commentary because of the limitations of cartoons.

Meme-ifying Politics

In Jonathan Dean’s article, “Sorted for Memes and Gifs: Visual Media and Everyday Digital Politics”, he discusses the “memeification” of politics, describing how “for many politically engaged citizens, politics is enacted in and through visual media cultures such as gifs, memes and other forms of shareable visual content”.

Issues Within The Political Comic Editorial Rooms

Political cartoons are outdated in numerous ways, including the lack of diversity in editorial rooms, potential room for censorship, and the amount of time needed to create one. Columbia Journalism Review reviewed demographic information about Pulitzer Prize winners from the last hundred years up until 2016. They found that only two women have ever won a Pulitzer for editorial cartooning. As of 2015, Washington Post reported that “not a single full-time staff political cartoonist on a major American daily newspaper is black”. Further, Darrin Bell became the very first African-American Pulitzer Prize winner for editorial cartooning in 2019. The lack of diversity in editorial cartoon rooms is a major limit in the potential of political cartoons to share cultural commentary from a varied perspective.

Where Memes Succeed Over Comics

For memes, there are no barriers for entry or limits placed on who can create a meme, making it inherently a more democratic medium. As for censorship, political memes don’t have to stifle the message they are conveying, while there are certain social and ethical boundaries that political cartoons cannot cross. In 2019, The New York Times had to issue an apology for a cartoon that was deemed anti-Semitic because it “portrayed a blind President Trump, wearing a skullcap, being led by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of Israel, drawn as a dog on a leash with a Star of David collar”. While offensive depictions of a race should not be allowed to be published, it’s undeniable that there is more freedom of speech through memes due to a lack of gatekeeping, causing it to be a double-edged sword. On one hand, a diversified set of voices get the opportunity to be heard, but on the other, hate speech is able to be spread on largely uncensored platforms. Lastly, since memes require less skill to craft than a political cartoon, memes can respond faster to politics than a cartoon can, potentially spreading further as well. Political comics are becoming a less relevant medium for political issues due both to the decline in print journalism and the rise in online meme culture.
